## Problem Statement
SMU students are not short on finance content. They are short on a structured way to practice, test recall under pressure, and know whether their answers would survive a real recruiting interview.

The current pattern is fragmented. Students ask seniors for resources, get scattered advice, and then have to guess whether they are ready. Some students ignore advice entirely, others exceed expectations once they follow a structured path. There is no stable middle. The product needs to create that middle.

## Demand Evidence
- Justin Hong said the financial modelling guide helped him learn far more than the financial modelling unit, and that the unit arrived too late to affect interview readiness.
- Students have told the mentor that the shared resources are more useful than course material.
- The recurring complaint is not "I need more content." It is "I need a better way to practice and know if I am actually ready."

## Status Quo
Today, students fend for themselves. They ask seniors for resources, get a pile of links, and try to piece together what matters.

The real cost is not just time. It is uncertainty. They do not know:
- what assessment modes to expect
- which theory matters most
- how to translate theory into interview answers
- whether their financial modelling knowledge will hold up in live recall

That uncertainty is what the engine should remove.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge
Target user: a motivated SMU student, like Justin Hong, who is preparing for finance interviews and wants a credible path, not a content dump.

The narrowest wedge: a well thought out syllabus outline plus interview-realistic practice questions, model answers, and answer pointers.

That is the thing someone would pay for this week.

## Constraints
- Copyright and continuity matter. If external providers disappear or change access, the preparation asset should not disappear with them.
- The career office needs an owned asset, not a rented dependency.
- The content does not need to be huge. It needs to be credible and sequenced.
- The engine has to feel like it was written by someone who understands recruiting, not by a generic study bot.
- WSP and BIWS already cover the generic global market. SMU needs a local, mentor-aligned layer on top.

## Premises
1. The core problem is not access to information, it is turning information into interview-ready recall under pressure.
2. The smallest valuable wedge is a syllabus plus realistic practice questions, model answers, and pointers on how to respond, not a full platform.
3. SMU wins by being locally specific and mentor-aligned, while WSP and BIWS own the generic global market.
4. Owning the content matters because it reduces copyright and continuity risk, and lets the career office control the long-term asset.

## Approaches Considered
### Approach A: Content-First Syllabus
Summary: Build a structured syllabus, topic by topic, with owned questions, model answers, and answer pointers. The career office controls the whole thing.
Effort: S
Risk: Low
Pros:
- Fastest path to something useful
- Easy for mentors to contribute to and review
- Solves ownership and continuity immediately
Cons:
- No active practice loop yet
- Weak if students want feedback
- Can feel like documents instead of a product
Reuses:
- Mentor knowledge
- Existing shared resources, rewritten into owned SMU content
- A simple CMS, markdown, or static site

### Approach B: Owned Practice Engine
Summary: Build the syllabus plus a practice bank where students answer realistic questions, see model responses, and get feedback on what a strong answer includes.
Effort: M
Risk: Med
Pros:
- Matches the actual pain: recall under pressure
- Creates a durable owned asset, not just a resource list
- Easier to expand later into assessments, analytics, and mentor workflows
Cons:
- Needs more product design up front
- Feedback quality depends on rubric quality
- Still requires ongoing content maintenance
Reuses:
- The syllabus from Approach A
- Mentor expertise as the source of truth
- Simple scoring/rubric patterns from interview prep products

### Approach C: Mentor-In-The-Loop Studio
Summary: Students practice on owned questions, mentors review submissions, flag weak spots, and update the bank based on what students miss.
Effort: L
Risk: Med
Pros:
- Best fit for the career office trust model
- Highest chance of producing genuinely good content over time
- Makes the "median outcome" problem visible
Cons:
- Harder to run
- Requires mentor time
- More workflow coordination
Reuses:
- Existing mentor advice flow
- The owned content corpus from A/B
- Existing career office relationships

## Recommended Approach
Choose Approach B.

It is the smallest product that actually changes student behavior. It solves the main pain, which is not content scarcity but whether a student can use that content under interview pressure. It also creates owned content that stays with SMU, instead of leaving the career office dependent on a vendor that could disappear or change terms.

Approach A is a good first slice, but it risks becoming a static library. Approach C is the long-term ideal, but it is heavier than the office needs on day one. B is the engine. Build that first.

## Open Questions

- Which interview formats matter most for SMU students, and how different are they by role?
- Who owns rubric quality, and how often do mentors review it?
- What is the launch surface, a private portal, a shared internal site, or something else?
- How much of the content should be visible before login versus inside the practice flow?

## Success Criteria
- A student can follow the syllabus without needing a mentor to explain the path.
- A student can practice a question, see a model answer, and understand why their own answer was weak.
- Mentors can add or revise a question without breaking the structure.
- The career office owns the content and can keep it alive even if external providers change.
- Students stop treating preparation as a pile of links and start treating it as a sequence.

## Distribution Plan
This should ship as an internal web service for SMU students and mentors.

Distribution needs:
- authenticated access for students, mentors, and career office staff
- a host that the career office can keep using without depending on a third-party prep vendor
- a publishing path for new modules and practice questions

If there is already a campus web platform, deploy into that. If not, set up a simple CI pipeline that publishes the service on merge to the main branch.

## Dependencies
- Initial module outline from a mentor or subject owner
- Agreement on which finance topics matter for recruitment
- A lightweight review process for practice questions and model answers
- A decision on hosting and authentication

## The Assignment
Build the first owned practice module with one topic, three realistic questions, model answers, and answer pointers. Then put it in front of a small group of students and watch where they get stuck.
